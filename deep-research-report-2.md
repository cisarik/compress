## Why prime-index plus diff does not automatically compress

A lossless compressor is an injective map on finite bitstrings, so it cannot shrink every possible input; modern compression work still states this point explicitly, and random data remains the canonical baseline that should not compress. Shannon’s framework ties compressibility to the statistics of the source, not to the use of an exotic coordinate system. So if you recode a block-value `x` into a different exact representation, you only win when that new representation makes the source **more statistically predictable** to the coder. A reversible reparameterization by itself is not enough. citeturn22view0turn16view0turn25view2

Your proposed map is something like this: choose the nearest prime `p_i`, then store `i` and `d = x - p_i`. The killer fact is prime density. The prime-counting function satisfies `π(x) ~ x / log x`, so the number of available prime anchors below `x` is smaller than `x` by roughly a factor of `log x`. Equivalently, the density of primes near `x` is about `1 / log x`, and the average prime gap near `x` is about `log x`. Those two facts almost perfectly cancel in coding terms: the index `i` saves you about `log2(log x)` bits relative to storing `x` directly, but the residual `d` typically needs about `log2(log x)` bits of dynamic range, plus sign handling and overhead. The nth prime asymptotic `p_n ~ n log n` is the inverse face of the same phenomenon. This is the central mathematical reason prime-index coordinates are not an automatic compression win. citeturn18view1turn18view0turn6view0turn23view0

There is a second structural problem: prime gaps are not just irregular, they are unbounded. PrimePages notes both that arbitrarily large prime gaps exist and that the “average gap” only describes local behavior. So any scheme that tries to preallocate a fixed residual budget for `d` will eventually need escapes, fallbacks, or special cases when the chosen prime neighborhood is too wide. Those escape mechanisms cost bits and complexity exactly where you most hoped for elegance. citeturn18view0

There is also a practical decode issue. To reconstruct `x` from `i` and `d`, the decoder needs the **exact** prime `p_i`, not merely an approximation. Research on the nth prime gives excellent asymptotic estimates and explicit upper and lower bounds, but those are still bounds and approximations; you must either serialize a prime table, run an exact prime generator/sieve, or do exact primality search around an approximation during decoding. That means the true project cost is not only bit-length but also model size, tables, and compute. citeturn6view0

The upshot is simple: “every integer is close to a prime” is true in a loose local sense, but “therefore it is shorter to describe” does not follow. Closeness to a sparse set only helps when the source distribution makes the residuals unusually concentrated or contextually predictable. Without that, the prime index and the prime residual just trade uncertainty back and forth. citeturn18view1turn18view0turn22view0

## Where symbolic regression is actually useful

This is the part where your background becomes highly relevant. Symbolic regression is not just curve fitting with constants; it searches over analytical forms as well as parameters. Schmidt and Lipson describe symbolic regression as an evolutionary-computation method that searches the space of mathematical expressions rather than fitting only coefficients of a pre-fixed form, and the modern PySR system likewise frames symbolic regression as searching analytic expressions while jointly minimizing prediction error and model complexity with a multi-population evolutionary algorithm. citeturn28view0turn22view1turn27view1

That matters because the scientifically promising version of your idea is **not** “discover a formula for primes that compresses everything.” It is “discover a compact deterministic predictor or anchor family that minimizes total code length on a chosen source class.” In that setup, primes become one basis family among many. You could let symbolic regression search anchor generators such as linear functions, polynomial/logarithmic forms, nth-prime approximants, prime-power variants, or hybrid expressions, then score each candidate on the actual number of bits needed to encode anchor choice, residuals, and model description. Recent symbolic-regression work has pushed exactly in this direction by using minimum description length as a principled way to combine fit and simplicity into one information-theoretic objective. citeturn22view2turn20search1turn20search6

That MDL perspective is the most important design choice for your notebook. Your fitness function should not be mean-squared error on reconstructed numbers. It should be something much closer to:

`fitness = bits_model + bits_headers + bits_indices + bits_residuals + λ * decode_time`.

In other words, the winning expression is the one that reduces **total description length**, not the one that merely predicts values numerically well. The MDL literature in symbolic regression is useful here precisely because it puts accuracy and structural simplicity in the same unit: information. citeturn22view2turn20search1

There is one more crucial connection to modern compression. Recent work on compression with predictive models emphasizes that a model becomes a lossless compressor when you combine its probabilities with arithmetic coding or ANS; the model supplies probabilities, and the entropy coder turns those probabilities into near-optimal bit lengths. So if you use symbolic regression, the natural role for it is as the **modeling front end**, not as the entire codec. Let symbolic regression discover a deterministic predictor or anchor map; then let ANS or arithmetic coding do the actual entropy coding of `i`, `d`, or whatever transformed residuals remain. citeturn22view0turn22view3

## A notebook program that can kill bad ideas quickly

A Jupyter notebook is the right medium, because this project needs disciplined falsification more than grand claims. Start with a very small family of exact transforms and measure **full** cost: compressed bits, model bits, table bits if any, decompression speed, and memory. Compression benchmarks such as the Canterbury Corpus exist precisely to evaluate lossless methods in a standardized way, and the Large Text Compression Benchmark explicitly counts decompressor size as part of serious comparisons. That is perfectly aligned with an MDL-style fitness. citeturn31view0turn31view2turn22view2

The first experimental battery should include four source types. Use the Canterbury Corpus because it was built as a benchmark for lossless compression methods. Use a large structured text benchmark such as enwik8 or its larger relatives from the Large Text Compression Benchmark. Use truly random bytes, because random data is the quickest sanity check for bogus “universal compression.” And add at least one domain-specific numeric source where anchor coding might plausibly help, such as timestamps, sorted identifiers, counters, or low-noise sensor streams. citeturn31view0turn31view2turn22view0

Inside that notebook, the earliest ablations should be intentionally boring. Compare nearest lower prime, nearest upper prime, nearest prime, and a non-prime baseline such as nearest power-of-two or a simple linear predictor. Compare fixed block widths like 8, 16, 24, and 32 bits. Compare absolute prime indices with delta-coded indices. Compare raw signed residuals with zigzagged residuals and a real entropy coder. If prime anchors do **not** beat very plain baselines on numeric data designed to favor them, you have learned something important quickly and cheaply. If they only help on one narrow source family, that is still a valid result; it just means the project is a specialized codec, not a universal one. citeturn18view1turn18view0turn22view3

Only after those baselines are stable should you bring symbolic regression into the loop. At that stage, the search space should be constrained hard: use primitives like `+`, `-`, `*`, protected division, `log`, `floor`, `round`, maybe `i log i`, maybe an nth-prime approximation term. Keep decode determinism sacred. Penalize expressions that need expensive exact-search cleanup around approximate anchors. And always charge the model for its own complexity, because benchmarks and MDL both punish “compressors” that quietly move cost into model description. citeturn22view1turn22view2turn31view2

## The best framing for the project

The most promising version of your idea is **domain-specific anchor coding**: take structured integer streams, predict an anchor from context, store a small residual, and let the entropy coder finish the job. In that setting, primes can be one candidate anchor family, but they should compete against other mathematically simple families rather than being assumed special from the start. This reframes the project from number mysticism into disciplined model discovery. citeturn22view0turn22view3turn22view2

The most novel version is probably **symbolic-regression-discovered anchors**. Instead of fixing `a_i = p_i`, let the system evolve an anchor law `a_i = A(i)` or even `a_t = A(context_t)` and judge it by total codelength. In that search space, prime-like terms, logarithms, floors, harmonic-style corrections, or other arithmetic ingredients can appear if they genuinely help. This is much closer to how successful symbolic regression is used in science: discovering compact, interpretable laws from data, while explicitly balancing accuracy and complexity. citeturn28view0turn22view1turn27view1turn22view2

The least promising version is using the explicit Riemann machinery itself in the codec’s inner loop. The site’s wave decomposition is beautiful because it exposes the deep relation between zeros and the weighted prime staircase, but it is not a cheap exact decoder for file blocks, and the site itself is careful not to oversell it as a direct next-prime formula. Use the screenshot as conceptual inspiration for “global irregularity rebuilt from simple components,” not as the literal computational primitive. citeturn4view0turn19view0
